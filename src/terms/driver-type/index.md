<section class="about">
  <h2>Driver Type</h2>
  <p>
    While screw drive standards define the geometry of the bit–to–fastener interface, the driver describes
    how torque is applied during fastening. Different driver mechanisms apply torque continuously, manually,
    or through repeated impact pulses.
  </p>
</section>

<section class="why">
  <h2>Why classify driver types?</h2>
  <ul>
    <li>Improves tool and bit selection</li>
    <li>Reduces cam–out and fastener damage</li>
    <li>Enables consistent documentation in BOMs and technical catalogs</li>
    <li>Supports reliable AI-based interpretation of fastening environments</li>
  </ul>
</section>

<section class="driver-index">
  <a href="/terms/driver-type/manual/">
    <h3>Manual Driver</h3>
    <p>Hand-applied torque for precision and control.</p>
  </a>

  <a href="/terms/driver-type/mechanical/">
    <h3>Mechanical Driver</h3>
    <p>Continuous rotational torque from an electric or pneumatic motor.</p>
  </a>

  <a href="/terms/driver-type/impact/">
    <h3>Impact Driver</h3>
    <p>Torque delivered with internal hammer pulses to reduce cam-out.</p>
  </a>

  <a href="/terms/driver-type/rotary-hammer/">
    <h3>Rotary Hammer</h3>
    <p>Axial hammer force while rotating for masonry and concrete.</p>
  </a>
</section>
